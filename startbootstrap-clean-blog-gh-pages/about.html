Beyond The Pitch

Soccer’s Impact on Global Socio-Economic Dynamics

Whether you call it “soccer” or “football” , at some point in your life you’ve been a player, a fan, or at least a spectator of The Beautiful Game. With more than 5 billion fans worldwide, soccer is the most popular sport on the planet; but it is much more than that. For example, just last year the US Women’s National Soccer Team reached a settlement that required equal pay for both male and female soccer players in all competitions, which marked a milestone towards gender equality worldwide.

Soccer is more than just 22 people running behind a ball. As Liverpool’s former coach Bill Shankly, once said: “Some people believe football is a matter of life and death. I am very disappointed with that attitude. I can assure you it is much, much more than that. ” Throughout the course of history, soccer has been used as an instrument to promote social change, a political weapon, a multi-billion- dollar revenue industry, and a tool for mass mobilization. And in the last century, FIFA, the international governing body of the sport, has overseen this world-changing endeavor.

FIFA, or the Fédération Internationale de Football Association, was founded in 1904 and is headquartered in Zurich, Switzerland. Its primary role is to regulate and promote soccer on a global scale. FIFA's responsibilities include organizing major international tournaments such as the FIFA World Cup, which is the most prestigious event in soccer. Additionally, FIFA sets and enforces the rules of the game, manages the transfer of players between clubs and countries, and works to develop and grow soccer at all levels, from grassroots initiatives to professional leagues.

Furthermore, FIFA plays a crucial role in shaping the socio-economic landscape of soccer by negotiating broadcasting rights, sponsorship deals, and revenue distribution among its member associations. As the governing body of the world's most popular sport, FIFA wields significant influence over the development, governance, and commercialization of soccer, making it a central figure in the global sporting landscape.

THE POWER TO SHAPE HISTORY

Throughout history, soccer has been used to promote distinct political ideals around the world. According to Simon Martin, a political anthropologist from the University of London, Mussolini used the Italian national team to showcase the strength and power of fascism. Italy hosted the 1934 World Cup and took advantage of the tourism and media attention to display the country’s prowess and style during the fascist governance.

Simon claimed that Italy’s victories of both the 1934 and 1938 World Cups demonstrated fascism's dominance and its reshaping of football to serve as a propaganda tool, influencing public opinion, infiltrating daily life, and constructing a national identity aligned with the regime's ideals. By strategically leveraging the sport's popularity and success on the international stage, authorities can influence public opinion, reinforce ideological narratives, and even display dominance over their political enemies. The use of soccer as a display of dominance was seen almost at the same time as Mussolini’s regime, during the Spanish Revolution.

In 1936, General Francisco Franco used the capital’s soccer team, Real Madrid, to show dominance over the Basque, Catalan, and Galician provinces. Real Madrid dominated the league for several years. By signing the best players in the world, they came on top of Athletic Club Bilbao, F.C Barcelona and R.C Celta de Vigo (the most important teams of said provinces), amongst others. By investing in soccer infrastructure and talent development, and by gaining national and international success, governments can project strength and power, potentially undermining political adversaries and bolstering their own geopolitical standing.



A country’s geopolitical standing depends largely on its depiction in the media. For example, in 1978 Argentina hosted and used the World Cup as a mean of diverting attention from its oppressive military regime and the atrocities committed during the Dirty War. Despite international calls for boycotts due to human rights abuses by Argentinian dictator Jorge Videla’s regime, the Argentinian government saw the tournament as an opportunity to bolster its legitimacy and image. According to Ken Rushworth, a researcher from the University of Manchester, Argentina went so far as to create temporal concentration camps for the regime’s political opponents, and to clear impoverished neighborhoods located near stadiums to keep them hidden from the media.

In addition, Argentina allegedly interfered in matches, all while the regime suppressed dissent and perpetrated violence. Ultimately, the World Cup served as a propaganda tool for the dictatorship, showcasing a facade of unity and celebration while atrocities continued unchecked, highlighting the exploitation of soccer for political gain. This instances of political corruption and violence in the World Cup have decreased – although not disappeared – over the years, but the tournament’s influence in global economics has only increased.



A MULTI-BILLION DOLLAR INDUSTRY


Every four years, FIFA publishes a comprehensive report of its financial statements. In their 2019- 2022 Investments/Expenses Report, FIFA summarizes its economic activity leading to the 2022 World Cup in Qatar. According to the report, FIFA dedicated a total of $1.8 billion to organize and market the tournament, which included a $440 million prize money pool, making the 2022 World Cup the most expensive tournament in the history of the sport.
FIFA announced that the final game between Argentina and France had a staggering 1.5 billion viewers worldwide. To put it into perspective, CBS’ broadcast of Super Bowl LVIII, which was the most watched television event in US history, had around 123.4 million viewers. The World Cup Final had 15 times more viewers than the most watched broadcast in US history. In other words, more than one out of eight people in the world was watching the game at the same time.

A broadcast reaching over 1.5 billion viewers possesses an unparalleled potential to shape society on a global scale. Such a massive audience provides an extraordinary platform for promoting brands, exponentially amplifying their visibility and consumer reach. Publicity campaigns can also transcend borders, reaching every corner of the world simultaneously and creating unprecedented levels of awareness and engagement. Likewise, political messages disseminated through such a broadcast could influence public opinion and potentially shape the course of international affairs.



The impact on the collective mindset of over a billion people cannot be overstated; it has the power to reshape societal norms, alter cultural perceptions, and drive significant shifts in global attitudes and behaviors. A broadcast of this magnitude not only serves as an information platform but also as a catalyst for societal change. The influence that such a tournament could have in society was recognized by diverse human rights organizations, who heavily criticized FIFA for awarding the tournament’s hosting privileges to Qatar.




Broader human rights issues including violations to the freedom of expression and association were brought up by the LGBT community after Qatar’s strict restriction on displays of affection by same- gender couples. The extreme heat of Qatar's climate also prompted scheduling adjustments, delays in stadium construction and infrastructure readiness, as well as environmental impacts like elevated energy consumption and carbon emissions.

As you can see, there is an intricate interplay between soccer and global socio-economic dynamics. Throughout history soccer has been used as a weapon of soft power, a multi-billion-dollar industry, and a platform for mass mobilization. Soccer, through the stewardship of FIFA, serves as more than just a game; it is a lens through which we can understand and address broader socio- economic issues.

By critically engaging with the complexities of soccer's influence on society, we can work towards a more sustainable future for the sport and its global community. As fans of the sport, it is our duty to recognize the importance and power of soccer and give it the best possible use: turning it into a platform for positive societal change.
